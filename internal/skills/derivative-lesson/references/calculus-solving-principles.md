# Calculus Solving Principles

## Domain Before Differentiation

State the function domain before differentiating whenever logarithms, denominators, roots, or piecewise definitions appear. Carry open endpoints and excluded values into every monotonicity interval and final range statement.

## Tangent Lines

For a differentiable function `y=f(x)` at a contact abscissa, use the sequence:

1. Compute the contact point, for example `(x1,f(x1))`.
2. Compute the slope `f'(x1)`.
3. Write `y-f(x1)=f'(x1)(x-x1)`.
4. Expand only when coefficient comparison or a second tangency condition needs it.

For a common tangent to `f` and `g`, give the two contact abscissas different names. Match slope and intercept separately; do not assume the contact abscissas are equal.

Do not compress a first common-tangent explanation into one large algebra step. Prefer this student-facing sequence:

1. Derive the tangent to `y=f(x)` at the first contact point.
2. Introduce the second contact point and derive the tangent to `y=g(x)` there.
3. State that both equations represent the same line, then match slope and intercept.
4. Obtain the requested coefficient expression and only then substitute the given contact value.

Keep the two tangent derivations parallel. Each should show the derivative, the slope at its own contact point, the point-slope equation, and the simplified line equation. Name the points, such as `P(x1,f(x1))` and `Q(x2,g(x2))`, and never infer `x1=x2` merely because the tangent line is common.

When two equations represent the same nonvertical line, state that fact before matching the coefficient of `x` and the constant term. This makes the system of equations motivated rather than appearing from nowhere.

## Parameterization Gate

When a problem asks for the range of a coefficient but the condition is generated by a moving contact point, introduce the contact abscissa as the independent parameter. Define a familiar auxiliary function such as `h(t)` and write `a=h(t)`. The letter is local notation, not a universal mathematical object; prefer `h` or `phi` over an uppercase letter that may be mistaken for a point name.

Reuse the symbol already present in the problem. If the statement names the contact abscissa `x1`, use `x1` in the derivation, bindings, slider, and parameter function. Do not rename it to `t` unless an actual symbol conflict requires a new name.

Use this order:

1. Derive the tangent from the first function.
2. Introduce the second contact abscissa.
3. Match slopes to express the second contact point in terms of the first contact abscissa.
4. Match intercepts to obtain the coefficient as a function of that same abscissa.
5. Define an auxiliary function such as `h(x1)` and analyze it on its complete mathematical domain.

Do not drag `a` independently after deriving `a=h(t)`; that would break the tangency condition.

## Monotonicity And Extrema

Factor the derivative when possible. List all critical points and domain boundaries, then determine the derivative sign on every interval. Distinguish local extrema from the global answer.

For a global range on an unbounded domain:

- compare all local minima and endpoint values;
- state the behavior at `+∞` and `−∞` when relevant;
- explain why no smaller value occurs outside the visible slider range.

The chart may confirm the result but is not the proof.

For a coefficient-range problem reduced to a one-variable auxiliary function, do not combine the whole proof into one or two dense cards. Prefer four student-facing steps:

1. Define the auxiliary function and state its complete domain.
2. Differentiate, factor the derivative, and solve for all critical points.
3. Determine the derivative sign on every interval and state the monotonicity.
4. Compare critical values and relevant endpoint or infinity behavior, then conclude the global range.

Keep these actions visually progressive: critical points appear in step 2, the sign band appears in step 3, and the final range band appears only in step 4.

## Student-Facing Step Discipline

- Write full card titles as an explicit mathematical action, object, and condition, for example `求曲线 y=f(x) 在点 P(x1,f(x1)) 处的切线方程`. Avoid conversational titles such as `由 f 和切点写切线`.
- Keep navigation labels compact but semantically aligned with the full title, for example `求 f 在 P 处的切线`.
- Distinguish notation precisely: `f` is the function name, `f(x)` is a function value or expression, and `y=f(x)` denotes the curve being discussed.
- Treat names such as `h(x1)` as local definitions, not universal expressions. State `令 h(x1)=...，则 a=h(x1)` before discussing its derivative or value range.
- Present parallel tangent derivations with the same four-line structure: derivative, slope at the contact point, point-slope equation, simplified line equation.
- In compact derivation rows, prefer mathematical connectors such as `∵` and `∴` over prose labels such as `因为`, `点斜式`, or `整理`. Explain the operation in the formula text when needed.
- When the next step differentiates a coefficient function, show both the factored/substitution form and its expanded polynomial form before differentiating.
- Keep derivative rules and the application of those rules in separate lines when the algebra is dense.
- Name intermediate meanings: contact slope, second contact point, parameter function, critical point, local minimum, global minimum.
- Separate algebraic jobs that students can verify independently. In a range proof, do not combine “solve the derivative equation”, “judge signs”, and “compare global values” in one card.
- Keep exact fractions such as `−1/3` and `5/27` in derivations and navigation labels.
- Use decimal values only for slider defaults or viewport bounds.
- Do not add conclusions the problem did not ask for merely because the renderer can display them.
